Glossary of General Neurodivergent and Autism Terminology

Red terms are considered outdated/disliked

Term Definition Shorthands
Neurodiversity a term representing the diversity of human brain fucntion. The term neurodiversity itself, contrary to popular belief is, not only used to describe people who are neurodivergent; the term includes both neurotypical and neurodivernet brain function, relating to the whole spectrum of brain variation. None
Neurodivergent having a brain that functions differently from the perceived societal norms of brain function. This includes people with neurodevelopmental variations, common ones being Autism, ADHD, and dyslexia and many more. However, the terms also includes epilepsy and traumatic brain injury. This term applies to an individual. ND
Neurodiversity this term holds the same meaning as neurodivergent, but refers to a group of neurodivergent individuals. You can think about it as: A single person themselves cannot be diverse, but a group can. None
Neurominority A group of neurodivergent individuals who are of the same neurological identity, (ie. a group of autistic people) in which their neurodivergence is an inseperable part of them and has faced considerable prejudice and discrimination. None
Neurotypical having a brain that funcyions in line with the percived societal norms of brain function. Essentially the opposite of neurodivergent. NT
Autism See this page for more details. ASD
Aspergers A diagnositic term no longer recognized in the DSM-5 that was previously used to refer to those with "less visible" autistic traits and challenges. The term was coined by Hans Asperger, a figure who was a nazi researcher. Autistic people generally do not like to use this term both because of the history and dividing effect the term provides. AD
Person First Language referring to a person with their "personhood" before their diagnostic label, (ex. "Person with Autism"). This language is disliked by the majority of the autistic community as we feel autism is an inseperable piece of us, and we are proud of it! However, some do prefer this language, so be sure to ask. None
Identity First Language referring to a person with their identitifying term first, (ex. "Autistic Person"), this language is liked by the majority of the autistic community due to it's implication that we are proud of our diagnosis "defining us." Our autistic brain shapes our personality, without autism we wouldn't be the same person. None
Special Interest An intensified focus on a certian topic and or activity. Unlike a typical "obsession" special interests help shape our world view. These are interests that we have had for over a year usually. Engaging in our special interests helps us recharge and brings immense joy, without doing so for awhile can cause distress. We want to talk about our special interests as much as possible, so ask us! None
Hyperfixation Hyperfixations, like special interests, are topics and or activities that neurodivergent people intensely focus on. Unlike special interests, hyperfixations often can start very quickly and end just as fast. Hyperfixations are not just autism specific, those with ADHD experience hyperfixations as well! None

Functioning Labels referring to someone as more "high functioning," or "low functioning." In terms of autism, the idea of functioning labels comes from the outdated linear spectrum model. The implication that one person can be "high or low functioning" is very harmful to both sides. If someone is called low functioning people will often assume they cannot think for themselves and treat their opinions with less respect. If someone is labeled high functioning they often won't be able to get as much support or even access to proper diagnosis since they "seem like they are fine," when in reality it's just masking.

Contrary to popular belief, we don't need these labels to help section off who needs which accomodations. Instead of labeling us, just ask what we need. 		High/Low Functioning
